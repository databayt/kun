# Share Economy

> **التضامن في الإنتاج، لا في الاستهلاك فقط.**
> *Solidarity in production, not in consumption alone.*
>
> — الحركة الوطنية للبناء والتنمية, *ورقة الاقتصاد التشاركي*

## The definition

Share economy at Databayt is one sentence:

> Every form of contribution is measurable, owned, and compensated; the value of the company is the sum of those contributions — never extracted from them.

The rest of this document is the lineage that makes it defensible and the vocabulary that makes it precise.

## What share economy is NOT

- **Not socialism.** Contributors are owners by measured contribution, not by membership.
- **Not charity.** The community fund is funded by revenue; recipients earn it through evidence of contribution.
- **Not crypto tokenomics.** No blockchain, no NFTs. Contribution Units are an accounting unit kept in a ledger.
- **Not customer-as-revenue-source.** Schools, vendors, clinics are partners we share value with.
- **Not VC velocity.** We bootstrap to profitability. Optional partnerships, no dilutive funding except on strict terms.
- **Not a license to fork-and-extract.** SSPL prevents closed competitors from absorbing the work without contributing back.

## The four references

This document binds four named intellectual references to the existing posture. Each contributes one specific thing.

### Akbar — Huquq (rights > property)

> *"The Western mode of property rights leads to rules and regulations, which results in intervention, bureaucracy, monopoly, and stratification, while the Islamic Sharia achieves the opposite through rights — Ḥuquq."*
> — Jamel Akbar, *Property Rights (Ḥuquq) and Civilizations* (2023)

**Jamel Akbar (جميل عبد القادر أكبر)** is a Saudi architect and theorist (King Saud University; MIT PhD 1984 under N. John Habraken; University of Dammam 1984–2016; currently Fatih Sultan Mehmet Vakıf University, Istanbul). His central project, ***قص الحق*** (*Qas al-Haq*, 1st ed. 2014, 2nd ed. 2022, 1,800pp), argues that the Islamic legal tradition organizes economic life around **حقوق (Ḥuquq, "rights")** rather than property as exclusion.

Western property rights → rules → bureaucracy → monopoly → stratification. Resources become inaccessible to those without the right permissions; production concentrates; outcomes stratify. Ḥuquq → rights-based access → individuals can start productive activities without permission from authorities, as long as they do not harm others or the environment.

**Where Akbar's framework operates in Databayt.** Three concrete imports:

**1. The three claims and five forms of submission.** Akbar's 1984 MIT thesis identifies **three claims** any party can hold over a productive resource: ownership, control, and use. These combine into **five Forms of Submission**:

| Form | Software example |
|---|---|
| **Unified (موحَّد)** | Solo developer on their own tool |
| **Dispersed (موزَّع)** | Three parties — owner, controller, end user |
| **Permissive (إذني)** | SaaS subscription (user pays to use what vendor owns + controls) |
| **Possessive (حيازي)** | Self-hosted OSS (user uses + controls code owned upstream) |
| **Trusteeship (وصاية)** | Foundation-stewarded code; the future waqf-track Community Pool |

All five forms exist in Databayt today (Hogwarts at King Fahad = Permissive; self-hosting school = Possessive; solo Souq contributor = Unified; sponsored Mkan build = Dispersed; future waqf pool = Trusteeship).

**2. The three mechanisms** through which Huquq-based systems produce just outcomes — and OSS already practices all three:

- **Collective solution-seeking** → pull requests, RFCs, issue discussions
- **Action precedes permission** → fork-then-PR culture; the OSS "show, don't ask" norm
- **Refinement through conflict** → code review iterations as productive friction

Akbar frames the built environment as *"an open laboratory in which all users' brains, given the chance to experiment with an element, will find the best solution."* The same is true of the codebase.

**3. Darar and dirar — the harm distinction.** The Prophetic tradition *"لا ضرر ولا ضرار"* (no harm, no return of harm) distinguishes two harm patterns:

- **Darar (ضرر)** = extractive harm — actor benefits at others' expense. **SSPL is structured to prevent darar** (a closed competitor reselling our code without contributing back).
- **Dirar (ضرار)** = gratuitous harm — actor harms without even benefiting. **Trademark policy is structured to prevent dirar** (bad-faith forks misusing our name with no upside to the actor).

The license layer (SSPL) and the brand layer (trademark policy) thus address two distinct harm categories, not one.

A closed proprietary competitor cannot replicate this without burning their existing business model. Akbar gives that Counter-Positioning argument an older, deeper grounding than Silicon Valley strategy literature.

### NMBD — production-solidarity

> *"في السودان، يوجد قيمة التضامن — ولكن بدلاً من التضامن في الاستهلاك فقط، دعونا نتضامن في الإنتاج."*
> — الحركة الوطنية للبناء والتنمية, *ورقة الاقتصاد التشاركي*

**الحركة الوطنية للبناء والتنمية (NMBD, the National Movement for Construction and Development)** is a Sudanese reform movement. We engage with its **economic-philosophical material**, drawing one principle from the *Participatory Economy Paper*: move solidarity from the consumption side of the economy to the production side.

Most contemporary frameworks for "community" or "solidarity" in business operate on the consumption side: discounts, group-buying, mutual aid, charity. Those redistribute what production has already created. They do not change who produces or how production is organized. NMBD's move is to ask: **who participates in production, and how is the value of their participation valued?**

**Where it lives in Databayt.** Already practiced — the doctrine names what is already true:

- Ahmed Baha at King Fahad Schools is a **partner, not a customer**. His feedback, case studies, roadmap input are contributions.
- The MENA-10 pilot cohort is a **partnership cohort, not a sales pipeline**. Pilot schools shape the platform.
- Contributors are **owners, not labor**. The CU spec measures contribution; ownership accrues to the producers.

Civilizational figures whose work informs this lineage in our reading: مالك بن نبي (Malek Bennabi) and ابو القاسم حاج حمد (Abu al-Qasim Hajj Hammad).

### Khedr — MENA-context equity methodology

**د. محمد حسام خضر (Dr. Mohammed Hossam Khedr)** is an Egyptian engineer and practitioner (Cairo University; MBA Universitat Autònoma de Barcelona 2017; DBA 2025; ~28 years of operating experience). Founder of multiple ventures including **Internet Plus** (still operating) and **ألعاب شمس** (Sun Games, acquired by a larger firm); Managing Partner at Endure Capital; active angel investor in MENA startups; consultant and content creator at "خضر و بزنس" (Khedr & Business). His book ***رائد الأعمال Inside Out*** (Dar Don, 2019, 30+ chapters) is the most-cited Arabic-language entrepreneurship reference; the Equity Split chapter (تقسيم حصص الملكية في المشروع) is the most-praised section.

**Khedr's central practical claim:** discuss and document partnership equity *before* the company starts operating — never after. The book frames this as the most common cause of company failure that he has personally seen, across hundreds of MENA startups.

**Five specific Khedr contributions Databayt borrows:**

| Contribution | How it applies in Databayt |
|---|---|
| **Two equity methodologies** (traditional vs startup) | Traditional methodology → Mudaraba contract for sponsored custom builds. Startup methodology → CU framework for ongoing contribution. |
| **Khedr Helicopter** (staged-commitment expansion) | MENA-10 pilot = stage 1; conversion-to-paid = stage 2; multi-school scale = stage 3. Each stage's commitment scales with previous-stage traction. |
| **Pre-launch equity contract** | Every Databayt partnership (sponsored build, multi-contributor block, joint product) gets a written equity/CU allocation agreement *before* the first commit. |
| **Partner departure protocols** | Bought-out vested CU at fair valuation; unvested returns to pool; structured non-compete cooling-off; IP and customer-relationship transfer documented upfront. |
| **Sharia-compliant non-compete templates** | Limited geographic scope, limited duration, compensated where they restrict livelihood, structured to avoid *gharar*. First Databayt contributor agreements (when MRR ≥ $3K triggers paid hire) follow this pattern. |

**Why we trust Khedr.** He is not an academic theorist; he is a practitioner who has been on every side of the table (entrepreneur, investor, mentor, consultant) over 28 years, and his frameworks were built in Cairo and Riyadh rather than imported from Silicon Valley. His Sharia-compliance work is grounded in the legal and cultural context his contracts actually operate in.

### Islamic economy primitives — the operational vocabulary

Four primitives, each scoped narrowly. We use these terms with discipline — each names a specific operational mechanism, not a generic principle. We do not claim full classical-fiqh conformance; we cite each for the principle it expresses.

#### مضاربة (Mudaraba) — silent partnership for sponsored builds

A two-party contract: capital provider (*rabb al-mal*) and working partner (*mudarib*). Profit by pre-agreed ratio; loss to capital provider unless *mudarib*'s negligence.

**Applies in Databayt only to sponsored custom-build contracts** — e.g., a school network commissions a custom Hogwarts customization. Does *not* apply to SaaS subscriptions, per-student pricing, or free pilots.

#### شِركة الأعمال (Shirkat al-A'mal) — partnership of labor

A partnership where the substance contributed is *work* rather than *capital*. Partners contribute services or labor toward a shared activity, and profit is divided by an agreed ratio that need not be equal and can be re-negotiated as the partnership matures.

This is the **precise Arabic-rooted term for the contributor-as-owner model.** The CU spec is its operational expression.

**Honest divergence.** Classical *shirkat al-a'mal* would distribute *both* profit and loss pro-rata to partners. The Databayt CU model **caps contributor downside**: operational losses are absorbed by the operating reserve, not redistributed. This is the right design for an early-stage company where most contributors cannot underwrite operational risk — but it is a divergence we name openly.

#### وقف (Waqf-track) — perpetual community pool

A *waqf* is a perpetual, irrevocable endowment dedicated to a specified beneficial purpose, administered by a custodian (*nazir*).

We pledge that **5% of Databayt revenue** will be administratively segregated into a Community Pool the moment MRR exists. The pool matures into a formal *waqf* instrument when three triggers all fire: (1) MRR ≥ $10K/month sustained for 12 months, (2) Databayt is registered as a legal entity in a jurisdiction compatible with a *waqf* (likely Sudan, Saudi, or UAE), (3) external counsel advises on the *nazir* arrangement. Until then: **"Community Pool (waqf-track)"**.

Purposes: open infrastructure, contributor scholarships, emergency support, community events and education in Arabic-speaking regions.

#### زكاة (Zakat-aligned Needs Fund)

*Zakat* in classical Islamic-fiqh terms is a religious obligation on individual Muslims, not on corporations. We adopt the **2.5% rate, the redistribution intent, and the principle of structural obligation** — but we call our mechanism the **"Needs Fund / صندوق العون"** to avoid conflation with *zakat* as a religious duty.

**Mechanism.** When Databayt has net profits, 2.5% of net profits goes to the Needs Fund. Recipient categories: contributors in conflict zones (Sudan war, Yemen, Gaza-region) whose work has been disrupted; contributors in medical/displacement emergencies; new contributors below the pay-floor whose situation requires interim support; education-cause grants for partner schools facing crisis.

#### Underlying constraints

- **No riba (ربا — interest extraction).** No interest-bearing lending or borrowing where avoidable.
- **No gharar (غرر — excessive uncertainty).** Contracts are precise; pricing is transparent; CU calculations are public and contestable.
- **No harm to commons (الضرر).** SSPL prevents extractive behavior; trademark policy prevents confusion; CU anti-gaming controls protect the contributor commons.

## How each reference maps to a mechanism

| Reference | Principle | Where it operates today | Q3 2026 formalization |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Akbar / Huquq** | Rights-based access > exclusion | SSPL on hogwarts; open-by-default | (license is the structural enforcement) |
| **NMBD / production-solidarity** | Contributors are partners | Partner-not-customer (Ahmed Baha, MENA-10) | (principle frames the partnership policy) |
| **Khedr / two equity methodologies** | Distinct allocation logic for traditional vs startup company types | (gap — CU is one-size-fits-all) | Multi-class CU schema (CU-Cap / CU-Lab / CU-Strat) |
| **Khedr / Helicopter (staged expansion)** | Stage-N commitment scales with stage-(N–1) traction | MENA-10 pilot = stage 1 | Stage 2 = conversion-to-paid; stage 3 = multi-school scale |
| **Khedr / pre-launch equity contract** | Document the partnership *before* operations begin | (gap — partnerships informal) | Standard pre-commit equity agreement template |
| **Khedr / partner departure** | Bought-out vested CU; unvested returns to pool; MENA non-compete | (gap — never tested) | Departure handbook in `databayt/revenue` (Q3 2026) |
| **Mudaraba** | Sponsored custom-build profit split | (ad hoc per project) | Standard Mudaraba contract template |
| **Shirkat al-A'mal** | Labor partnership: contributors as owners | Full CU spec | CU pool sizing, founder reserve, operating reserve |
| **Waqf-track** | Perpetual community pool | Pledge only (no MRR yet) | Activation triggers; *nazir* arrangement; beneficiary policy |
| **Zakat-aligned Needs Fund** | 2.5% on net profits for need | Pay-floor (different beneficiaries) | Beneficiary policy; trigger conditions |
| **No riba / gharar / harm** | Constraint on product/financial decisions | SSPL, transparent pricing, no interest-bearing credit | Pricing review checklist; contract template review |

## What this document is and is not

**Is**: positioning, posture, intellectual lineage, captain context for share-economy decisions, reading list for new contributors.

**Is not**:

- **Not the operational spec.** CU formulas, contribution matrix, $45/hr floor, governance rules, founder reserve %, multi-class CU schema, conversion-to-cash mechanics — none live here. The measurement layer lives in [`hogwarts/content/docs-en/shared-economy.mdx`](https://github.com/databayt/hogwarts/blob/main/content/docs-en/shared-economy.mdx); the operational layer is being formalized for Q3 2026.
- **Not a Sharia-compliance certification.** We name the Islamic primitives as posture and vocabulary. Compliance review of specific products and contracts is a separate professional process.
- **Not a fatwa.** We are a software company, not a religious authority.
- **Not a political document.** NMBD references are economic-philosophical, not political.
