## Decision

When referring to Hogwarts internally or externally, the canonical framing is "the win horse" or "flagship" or "pilot path" — NOT "the money maker," "cash cow," or "revenue driver."

## Context

- Hogwarts is the first product to a paying customer (King Fahad Schools).
- It's tempting to talk about it as the revenue path because it is.
- But the framing creates a culture: "money maker" → extract value; "win horse" → build excellence and the win follows.
- Bill Walsh: *"The score takes care of itself when you take care of the effort that precedes the score."* If we frame Hogwarts as a money maker, we manage to revenue. If we frame it as the flagship, we manage to quality and reputation.
- Internal language compounds. External language positions.

## Premortem (retrospective)

- *"It failed because contributors thought we were doing aspirational marketing."* — Hasn't happened; the framing is consistent with Tier 1 principle "Quality over speed."
- *"It failed because the team forgot the convention and 'money maker' crept back."* — Mitigated by capturing here and in PRINCIPLES.md.

## Expected outcome

- **Success looks like**: Internal and public language consistently uses "win horse" / "flagship" framing. Investor pitches, sponsor outreach, content all align.
- **Failure looks like**: Mixed messaging — sometimes "money maker," sometimes "win horse" — confuses the brand.
- **Probability of success (at decision time)**: 0.9
- **Reasoning**: Low-cost behavior change with clear payoff; just needs reinforcement.

## Alternatives considered

1. **No internal framing at all (let people use any language)**: Rejected — language drift creates positioning drift.
2. **Use "money maker" since it's accurate**: Rejected — accurate doesn't mean strategic.

- Owner: Abdout
- Due: ongoing
- Next checkpoint: any time the team uses the wrong framing
